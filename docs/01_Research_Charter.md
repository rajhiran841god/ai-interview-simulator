# Research Charter — v1

**Project:** AI Interview Intelligence Platform (working name)
**Phase:** 01 — Problem Discovery
**Status:** Locked
**Date:** July 2026

---

## Objective

Determine whether "lack of realistic practice with objective evaluation" is
the real, primary driver of underperformance in MBA campus placement
Personal Interviews (PI) — or whether the true bottleneck lies elsewhere
(shortlisting, technical knowledge, interview volume, GD/WAT performance,
etc.).

This phase answers one question only: **is the problem real, for this
population, at this stage?** It does not test solutions, pricing, or
willingness to pay. Those are later, separate experiments (Round 2 and
Round 3 — see Problem Statement doc for the round split).

## Research Questions

1. How do MBA students preparing for campus placement PIs actually prepare
   today (behavior, not opinion)?
2. What happens when their preparation meets the real interview — where
   does it hold up, where does it break down?
3. Do students know, in the moment, whether they are "ready"? How do they
   judge that?
4. When students underperform or get rejected, do they understand why?
5. What do outside observers (recruiters, placement officers) see as the
   actual differentiator between successful and unsuccessful candidates?

## Target Participants

| Group | Count | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| MBA students (various specializations, LBSIM + others) | 8 | Primary population — deliberately spread across performance levels (multiple offers, repeat rejections, skipped placements) and specializations (Marketing, Finance, HR, Ops) |
| Engineering students | 4 | Outside triangulation — is this an MBA-specific pattern or a general campus placement pattern? |
| Recent graduates (placed within last year) | 4 | Retrospective accuracy — less recency bias contamination than current final-years, more reliable memory of what actually happened |
| Placement officers / TPO | 2 | Aggregate, cross-student pattern recognition |
| Recruiters | 2 | External, outcome-linked judgment of what actually separates candidates |
| **Total** | **20** | |

Note: the MBA-student subset (8) is the group the core hypothesis is
about. The other 12 are triangulation, not dilution — do not let them
substitute for a real, dedicated MBA sample.

## Method

- Live conversation (call or in-person), not a written survey. This stage
  is about discovering unknown variables, not measuring known ones —
  surveys are the wrong tool here.
- Recorded with consent; notes only if participant declines.
- Round 1 only: fact-and-recollection questions. No solution questions,
  no pricing questions. See Discovery Interview Guide.
- One consistent template per interview (see Interview Repository format).

## Bias Controls

- Interviewers must not have hands-on experience with competitor products
  before completing their assigned interviews (contamination control).
- Bottleneck tagging (Readiness / Resume / Technical Knowledge / Volume /
  Other) happens only after all 20 interviews are complete and read in
  full — not interview-by-interview — to reduce confirmation bias.
- Deliberately include participants outside the founder's existing
  friend/classmate network to avoid an agreeable-sample bias.
- Separate every finding into **Fact** (observed behavior, specific
  events) vs. **Interpretation** (the participant's own theory about why
  something happened).

## Evidence Gate — Exit Criteria for Phase 1

This phase is complete when **all** of the following are true:

- [ ] 20 interviews conducted and logged in the Interview Repository
- [ ] Every interview tagged with a primary bottleneck category
- [ ] A synthesis document written identifying recurring themes, with
      frequency counts (not vibes) — e.g., "12 of 20 participants
      described X"
- [ ] The falsification condition (below) has been explicitly evaluated
      against the data — not skipped

## Falsification Condition

The core hypothesis — *students underperform due to a lack of realistic,
evaluated practice* — is considered **not supported** if interviews show
that the primary, most frequently cited bottleneck is something other
than readiness: e.g., resume shortlisting, technical/domain knowledge
gaps, or simply not getting enough interview opportunities.

If that happens, this problem statement will be revised or rejected
rather than defended.

## Timeline

- Days 1–2: Finalize interview guide (this document + guide are treated
  as locked once interviews begin — no scope changes mid-round)
- Days 3–7: Conduct all 20 interviews
- Days 7–8: Synthesis and bottleneck tagging
- Exit: Move to Gate 2 (Competitor Product Teardown) synthesis, then
  Gate 3 (MVP definition)

## Explicitly Out of Scope for This Phase

- Any question about willingness to pay
- Any question that names or implies a solution ("would AI help you...")
- Product scope decisions
- Architecture or technical planning
- Group Discussion (GD) research — PI only, per locked problem scope
